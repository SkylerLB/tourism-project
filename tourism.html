Beirut Downtown:

Downtown Beirut is the central district of Beirut, with its outdoor cafes restaurants and high end designer stores.
The inner area around Nejmeh Square is pedestrian-only. Ashrafieh is to its East, while Hamra, Ras Beirut, Manara and Rawcheh to its West.
Getting to downtown from other parts of Beirut or one of the bus stations is easy. Simple hop in any taxi/Service and ask for either "Downtown" or "Solidere"
Navigating around Downtown can be tricky, as people often rely on landmarks rather than street numbers. It's important to familiarize yourself with key landmarks in the area. Using a geolocation service like PinLiban can help you track and note exact locations. Fortunately, Downtown is compact and can easily be explored on foot.
A great places to visit:

  • Nejmeh Square The recently renovated central square, with the famous clock tower built by Ottomans.
  • Martyr's Square synonymous with political demonstrations, Martyr's Square has a very rich history, and is home to the bullet-holed martyr's statue. Occasionally out-door art exhibitions are featured here.
  • Solidere District The French Mandate historic city center, which was destroyed during the civil war, and recently renovated and transformed into a chic high-end shopping and cafe quarter.


Raouché:

Raouché (Arabic: الروشة, romanized: ar-Rawše) is a residential and commercial neighborhood in Beirut, Lebanon.
It is known for its upscale apartment buildings, numerous restaurants, and cliff-side cafés that line Avenue de Paris, which forms part of the Corniche Beirut.
The corniche or the wide, seaside sidewalk of Avenue de Paris is popular on weekends and evenings where strollers and joggers crowd the pavements.
Pigeon Rock at sunset
Off the coast of Raouché, there is a natural landmark called the Pigeons' Rock (also known as the Rock of Raouché).
Located at Beirut's westernmost tip, the two huge rock formations, which stand like gigantic sentinels, are a popular destination for locals and visitors alike.

Hamra:

Hamra Street (Arabic: شارع الحمراء) is a major thoroughfare in Beirut, Lebanon, located in the Hamra neighborhood. Known as one of Beirut’s primary economic and diplomatic hubs, it was historically the center of intellectual activity in the 1960s and 1970s, thanks to its many sidewalk cafes and theaters. Before 1975, it was considered the trendiest area in the city. However, in the post-war period, other areas like Rue Monot, Rue Gouraud, and Rue Verdun have gained more prominence. In the mid-1990s, the Municipality of Beirut revitalized Hamra Street to attract tourists throughout the year. It was once dubbed Beirut's "Champs Elysées," popular with tourists, particularly Americans, Europeans, and wealthy Arabs. Hamra lies directly on the West of Downtown Beirut. Hail a taxi or walk to the short distance.
Expect traffic jam from 9am to 9pm, so let your taxi or your van drop you at the entrance of the area, and keep walking!

North Forest:

North Forest, much of which is a nature reserve. Located at the extreme end of the Mount Lebanon range in the Akkar area, Qamouaa is considered an important tourist centre. The surrounding heights rise to 2,454 meters and there are forests that are remarkable because of the abundance of such trees as junipers, hairy oaks, cypresses, cedars and Cilician firs. Judging by the cedars to be found there, geologists say that these historic forests are at least a thousand years old. The plains of Qamoua lie at 1,450 metres altitude in a sort of depression between the mountains. Qammoua is the most important forest region of Lebanon. It covers 30 kilometers by 25, from the valley of Wadi Gehanam to the sources of Kobbayat, and from the source of Fnaydik to that of the Nassarahs of Koubayat. This forest has more than 10 million trees of fifty different species, the most important of these including the junipers, cedars, and Cilician firs. The forest has suffered considerable damage throughout its history, first of all at the hands of the British, who cut down a great number of the trees in order to use their trunks as sleepers under the railway linking Lebanon’s North and South.



Qleyaat Airport:

The Kleyate Airport, which was known as such after the Civil War, became the site for a significant parliamentary meeting in 1989. During this meeting, the Lebanese Parliament agreed on the *Taaif Agreement*, a pivotal accord aimed at ending the Civil War and improving Lebanon's relationships with its neighboring countries. The Taaif Agreement played a key role in the political and social reconstruction of Lebanon after the conflict.

Baalbak:

Baalbek is a city located east of the Litani River in Lebanon's Beqaa Valley, about 67 km northeast of Beirut. It is the capital of Baalbek-Hermel Governorate. In Greek and Roman times Baalbek was also known as Heliopolis. In 1998 Baalbek had a population of 82,608, mostly Shia Muslims, followed by Sunni Muslims and Christians.
The Ancient Aliens show suggests that the ruins at Baalbek, specifically in the Bar Car Valley of eastern Lebanon, go beyond their well-known Roman and Greek architecture, such as the grand temples built to honor gods like Zeus. The site is believed by archaeologists to be over 9,000 years old, originally sacred to the Canaanite god Baal. However, the show takes this a step further, proposing that the massive stone foundation beneath the Roman structures might have been used as a platform for space travelers, speculating that it could have been a landing pad for ancient extraterrestrial visitors. This theory ties into the broader narrative of ancient astronaut theorists, who believe that the advanced technology and monumental scale of ancient structures like Baalbek may indicate extraterrestrial influence.



Ms. Khawla's place in Baalbek:

Ms. Khawla's shrine in Baalbek is a significant religious site for Muslims and an important part of religious tourism in the region. It attracts pilgrims seeking blessings, prayers, and spiritual connection. The questions surrounding the shrine's location in Baalbek—why Ms. Khawla's grave is situated there and what brought her to the Bekaa Valley—are part of its historical and religious mystique. According to historical accounts, after the martyrdom of Imam Hussein in Karbala, his sisters, daughters, and women of his family endured a long and arduous journey from Karbala to Kufa and then to Damascus. As part of this journey, they passed through several regions, including Aleppo, Hama, and Homs, eventually reaching Lebanon. It is believed that Ms. Khawla, along with the other women and family members of Imam Hussein, passed through Baalbek and Marja Ras al-Ain, where the Ras Imam Hussein Mosque is located. The discovery of the shrine and its significance to religious tourism raises questions about its historical context and the state's involvement in promoting it, especially given its role in attracting pilgrims.

Beqaa Valley:

The Beqaa Valley, located in eastern Lebanon, is the country's most important farming region. Stretching 120 kilometers (75 miles) long and 16 kilometers (9.9 miles) wide, it lies between Mount Lebanon and the Anti-Lebanon mountains. Known for its Mediterranean climate, the valley experiences snowy winters and dry, warm summers. Rainfall is limited due to the rain shadow effect from Mount Lebanon. Two major rivers, the Orontes and the Litani, originate in the valley, with the Litani flowing into the Mediterranean Sea. The region also has a significant agricultural industry.



Al - Quraoun:

The Al-Qaraoun Dam, also known as the Albert Niqash Dam, is a rock-filled dam in Lebanon. It stands 60 meters high and stretches about 1,090 meters long, with a maximum width of 162 meters. The dam's total volume is approximately 2 million cubic meters. Its structure features a paved rock front layer for stability. To prevent leaks, the dam includes reinforced concrete tiles, which range in thickness from 50 centimeters at the bottom to 30 centimeters at the top, covering an area of 47,000 square meters. These tiles are integral to the dam's construction and water retention system.

Aley:

Aley is a major city in Lebanon and the capital of the Aley District. It is the fourth-largest city in the country, located on Mount Lebanon, 15 km from Beirut on the highway to Damascus. Known as the *"Bride of the Summer Resorts"* due to its cool climate, Aley is also called the *"Capital of the Mountain"* and the *"Lebanese City of Fog"* because of its frequent mountain fog. Aley is a key tourist destination in Lebanon and the Middle East, attracting visitors, especially from the Persian Gulf, for its shopping, dining, and vibrant summer atmosphere. The historic *Souk Aley* is a bustling boulevard lined with palm trees, red-roofed stone houses, street cafés, outdoor restaurants, nightlife spots, antique shops, and boutiques. The city is also home to a well-known casino, public swimming pools like *Piscine Aley* and *Country Club*, and luxury hotels such as *Highland* and *Golden Tulip*, which features a bowling arena, swimming pool, and spa. Additionally, Aley boasts shopping centers like *Aley Center* and a variety of Lebanese and international restaurants.



Sawfar:

Sawfar, also spelled Saoufar or Sofar, is a village in the Aley District of Mount Lebanon. Located at an altitude of 1,320 meters, it lies along the main road connecting Beirut and Damascus. The village is home to the summer residence of the French Embassy in Lebanon and the renowned *Chateau Bernina* hotel, which offers stunning views of Lamartine Valley and Kneise Mountain. Sawfar experiences heavy snowfall in winter, with snow depths exceeding one meter after strong storms, and temperatures dropping below zero in December and January. In contrast, its moderate and dry summers make it a popular retreat for Lebanese residents escaping the coastal humidity. The village is also a favored summer destination for Arab tourists due to its peaceful atmosphere and pleasant climate.

Miziara:

Miziara (also spelled Meziara, Arabic: مزيارة) is a town in the Zgharta District of North Lebanon. It consists of four villages: Miziara, Harf Miziara, Houmeis, and Sakhra. The town is home to notable religious sites, including Our Lady of Miziara, Mother of Mercies, and St. Elias Shrines, as well as *Hotel Miziara*, the village’s first hotel. According to historian Boutros Béchara Karam, the Naamtallah Néhmé family originally resided in Rishtaamout before moving to Houmeis, a ruined village within present-day Miziara. With permission from the Shiite Hamadeh rulers of Jebbet Bsharri, the family settled in the area, which they found particularly appealing. Youssef, son of Naamtallah Néhmé, is regarded as the founder of Miziara, as documented by historians..



Arez El Rab Jaj:

**Arez el Rab - Jaj: A Hidden Gem in Lebanon** Arez el Rab in Jaj is a breathtaking natural destination, ideal for nature lovers and hiking enthusiasts. The site is home to ancient cedar trees, some of which are several hundred years old and can reach heights of approximately 35 meters. These majestic trees symbolize strength and resilience, making them a significant part of Lebanon’s cultural and natural heritage. The cedars of Lebanon (*Al Arz Lebanon*) are renowned worldwide, often referred to as the "Cedars of the Lord" (*Arz al Rab*). They provide shade and an awe-inspiring landscape, offering visitors a deep connection to nature. The beauty of these trees extends beyond nature, inspiring art and culture, as seen in the cedar tree artwork crafted from Bsharre’s cedar wood. Whether for exploration, relaxation, or admiration of Lebanon’s national symbol, Arez el Rab in Jaj is a must-visit destination.



Tannourine Cedar Forest Nature Reserve:

The Tannourine Cedar Forest Nature Reserve created by virtue of Law No. 9 on February 25, 1999. The reserve is managed by a committee in cooperation with the Ministry of Environment. The reserve protects the largest and most dense cedar forest of cedar in Lebanon (625ha). The cedars accounts for more tha n 80% of the total number of trees exceed 2,500,000 trees. Other trees that can be found are oaks, junipers and maples living all together. The unique geography and topography of the forest protected the forest from human exploitation. Since 2006, the reserve has been declared an "important bird area (IBA). The reserve constitutes an ecological and scientific niche of plant and animal biodiversity

Nabatieh City:

**Nabatieh: The Cultural and Economic Hub of Southern Lebanon** Nabatieh, also spelled Nabatîyé, is the capital of the Nabatieh Governorate in southern Lebanon. It serves as a vital economic and cultural center for the Jabal Amel region. While Lebanon has not conducted an official census since the 1930s, population estimates for Nabatieh have varied widely. A 2006 estimate suggested a population of around 100,541, making it one of Lebanon’s largest cities at the time. However, later estimates in 2013 placed the population at approximately 36,593, ranking it as the 11th largest city in the country. Despite fluctuations in population estimates, Nabatieh remains a crucial administrative, economic, and cultural hub. It is known for its active markets, historical significance, and vibrant local traditions..



Jabal El Sheikh:

**Mount Hermon: The Sacred Peak of the Anti-Lebanon Range** Mount Hermon, also known as Jabal El Haramoun or Jabal El Sheikh, is the highest peak in the Anti-Lebanon mountain range, spanning Lebanon, Syria, and the Israeli-Palestinian territories. Revered since antiquity, the mountain is home to numerous archaeological sites and religious landmarks. With over 70 biblical references, Mount Hermon holds deep spiritual significance. Some traditions suggest that Jesus Christ’s transfiguration took place atop this mountain. Today, despite geopolitical challenges, the western Lebanese slopes of Mount Hermon continue to be a site for religious pilgrimages and interfaith rituals, reflecting the region’s diverse religious heritage. The shared spiritual reverence for Mount Hermon fosters inter-sectarian connections, reinforcing its role in both local and national identity.



Qalaat el-Shekif:

Beaufort or Belfort Castle, known locally as Qal'at al-Shaqif (Arabic: قلعة الشقيف, romanized: Qalʾāt al-Shaqīf)[1] or Shaqif Arnun, is a Crusader fortress in Nabatieh Governorate, Southern Lebanon, about 1 kilometre (0.62 mi) to the south-south-east of the village of Arnoun. There was a fortification on the site before it was captured by Fulk, King of Jerusalem, in 1139 and construction of the Crusader castle probably began soon after. Saladin captured Beaufort in 1190, but 60 years later Crusaders re-took it. In 1268 Sultan Baibars finally captured the castle for the Islamic forces. Beaufort provides one of the few cases in which a medieval castle proved of military value and utility in modern warfare as well, as its late 20th-century history shows.

Naqoura:

**Naqoura: A Coastal Town with Historical and Strategic Significance** Naqoura (Arabic: الناقورة) is a small coastal town in southern Lebanon, known for its historical and strategic importance. Since March 23, 1978, it has served as the headquarters of the United Nations Interim Force in Lebanon (UNIFIL). Historically, Naqoura was described by 19th-century travelers such as Victor Guérin, who noted its fertile landscape, ancient ruins, and a thriving community of around 400 Metawileh. The village, situated near the historic Ladder of the Tyrians, was known for its olive groves, fig trees, and palm gardens. The 1881 Survey of Western Palestine also highlighted its abundant water sources and agricultural wealth. Today, Naqoura remains a key location due to its proximity to the Lebanese-Israeli border and its role in regional peacekeeping efforts.



Rihan:

**Al Rehan: A Scenic Mountain Village in Southern Lebanon** Al Rehan is a picturesque village nestled in the Jezzine District of southern Lebanon, located on the slopes of Al Rehan Mountain. The village is surrounded by majestic mountain peaks and dense pine and oak forests, offering breathtaking natural beauty. ### **Geography & Location** - **Altitude:** 740 to 1,605 meters above sea level - **Distance from Beirut:** 83 km - **Neighboring Villages:** Aramta, Sujod, and Ayshiya - **Panoramic Views:** Mountains beyond Lebanon’s borders can be seen from Al Ksayri ### **Name & Significance** The village and its namesake mountain derive their name from the **Basil plant (Rehan in Arabic)**, which grows abundantly in the region. ### **Population & Migration** Al Rehan has an estimated population of **5,000 people of Lebanese descent**, with many emigrants residing across the **Middle East, Africa, North and South America, Canada, and Europe**. Al Rehan is a serene destination, especially in the summer, attracting visitors who seek tranquility and scenic landscapes.
